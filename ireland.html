Carrauntoohill

Standing at 1,038.6 meters (3,407 feet and 6 inches), Carrauntoohill is Ireland's highest mountain and is located in the MacGillycuddy's Reeks mountain range.

There are many ways to climb Carrauntoohill, including:

  • The Devil's Ladder
  • Brother O' Shea's Gully
  • The Caher Trail
  • Coomloughra Horseshoe Loop

Each with their own challenges and lengths. From the Devil's Ladder with steep, scree-laden slopes to the Coomloughra Horseshoe which brings you to each of the 3 nearby peaks, there's a route for everyone.

Mangerton

Mangerton, also known as The Long Haired Mountain, is located in County Kerry, in the Mangerton Mountain Group. It stands at a height of 838 metres (2,749 feet), and is the highest in its mountain range. It lies within Killarney National Park and on it's slopes there sits a corrie or cirque, called the Devil's Punchbowl which is an attractive tourist destination. It is often covered with a blanket of snow especially during the Winter months.

It's slopes used to be part of a historical 13th-century battle between the Gaelic forces known as the Mac Cárthaigh and the Norman Forces known as the Fitzgeralds.

Torc

Located in the Killarney National Park, Torc Mountain is an extremely popular route with tourists and locals alike, due to its simple ascent with a path of wooden sleepers, and the spectacle of its waterfall. At the summit which is 535 meters (1755 feet) above sea level there is a magnificent view of the popular Muckross House and the surrounding Killarney town.

Torc gets its name from the Irish word Torc meaning Boar and it is said that an mystical boar was slain here by Irish legendary hero, Fionn mac Cumhaill who occurs in many other Irish mythological stories.

Croagh Patrick

Croagh Patrick is an important pilgrimage site located in Clew Bay in County Mayo. It has been seen as a holy mountain for thousands of years. It became associated with St. Patrick who is said to have fasted there for 40 days on the summit. It is climbed by thousands of people every year on Reek Sunday, the last Sunday of July. This custom has been around since at least the Middle Ages.

Its name comes from the Irish Cruach Phádraig meaning St. Patrick's stack, but it is known locally as 'The Reek'.
